PhD in Computer Science @ Illinois in the area Scientific Computing and Numerical Analysis

Are you interested in numerical algorithms? How about topics such as finite elements, multigrid solvers, integral equations, spectral element methods, fast algorithms, large-scale applications, or high-performance parallell algorithms?

You can purse studies in scientific computing and numerical analysis in the Computer Science Department in topics ranging from computation to mathematical theory and with application in Computer Science and more broadly in Engineering and the Physical and Social Sciences.

How To Apply:  Process. Tips.

  • Applications are due in December
  • Applicants must submit through the Department of Computer Science, which starts with an online application through the Graduate College.
  • Tip #1: Pursue Letters of Recommendation (Step 4) from someone in the area of Scientific Computing, Numerical Analaysis, or High-Performance and Parallel Computing.
  • Tip #2: Tell us about your specific interests in Scientific Computing in your Statement of Purpose (Step 5).
  • Tip #3: Select "Scientific Computing" as an area of interest when you fill out the Department's interest form (Step 6). In addition, try to identify some faculty whose research sounds interesting! (also step 6)

What To Expect:  Curriculum. Summer Internships. Community of peers.

The PhD requires 64 or 96 hours of credit, depending on whether you enter with a masters or a bachelors degree. The specific Program of Study (POS) is felxible, but generally our students take Numerical Analyais (CS450), the 500-level courses in our area, and some courses in parallel computing, theory, and engineering. In addition, the Computational Science and Engineering (CSE) Certificate Option is often pursued.

We typically have around 20 graduate students in Scientific Computing (check out our impressive alumni over the last several decades!) Our goal is a fun and positive atmosphere in Scientific Computing to help you take classes, study for the qualifying exam, and collaborate on research toward your PhD.

A great part of our program is the close connections with the national laboratories. During the summers you'll find many of our students collaborating or visiting places such as Argonne in Chicago, Lawrence Livermore in CA, and Sandia and Los Alamos both in New Mexico.
